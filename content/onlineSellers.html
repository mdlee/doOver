Online Sellers

This case-study comes from the youtube channel 3Blue1Brown, and involves comparing the positive ratings of three online sellers. The first seller has a 100% score, based on 10 positive ratings from 10 reviews. The second seller has 96% score, based on 48 positive ratings from 50 reviews. The third seller has a 93% score, based on 186 positive ratings from 200 reviews. The research question is to infer from these data which seller has the highest probability of providing a positive buying experience.

Inferring rates

Formally, the counts of positive ratings can be written , , and for the three sellers, and the total number of reviews can similarly be written , , and . A basic statistical model assumes that these data are generated by an underlying rate of positive experiences, denoted for the th seller. If this rate is constant across the review period, and all of the buying experience that generate reviews are independent, then the counts of positive ratings will follow a binomial distribution

for each seller.

As a starting point, it also seems reasonable to assume that the underlying rate of positive experience is equally likely to be between 0 and 1 for each seller. This corresponds to the uniform prior

The corresponding graphical model is shown below. The underlying rates are shown as a circular and unshaded node because they are continuous and unobserved (or latent). The counts are shown as a square and shaded node because they are discrete and observed. The number of reviews are also shown as square and shaded because they are also discrete and observed. In addition, however, the node is double-bordered, to indicate that it is deterministic rather than stochastic. This is because the is defined in terms of being sampled from a distribution in the model, but the are fixed. The graph structure indicates that the counts depend on the rate and the number of reviews. Finally, the plate encompassing the entire graph means that the binomial model is being applied independently to each seller.

The JAGS model onlineSellers_1_jags.txt implements the model, and makes inferences about the posterior distribution of the rates based on the and data. 

xxxxxxxxxx
# onLineSellers_1
# underlying rate inference
model{
  for (i in 1:nSellers){
    # Data
    k[i] ~ dbin(theta[i], n[i])
    # Prior
    theta[i] ~ dunif(0, 1)
  } 
}

The MATLAB script onLineSellers_1.m supplies the data to the model, and generates the plot of the posterior distributions shown below. The height of each posterior distribution at each possible rate shows the relative probability that the rate generated the observed rating data for each seller, according to the data-generation method assumed by the model.

The posterior distribution for the first seller shows that the underlying rate of positive reviews could be anywhere from about 0.6 up to the maximum of 1.0, with larger values in this range being more likely. The posterior distribution for the second seller shows that the underlying rate about be anywhere between about 0.8 and 1.0, with the most likely values being in the middle of that range. Finally, the posterior distribution for the third seller shows rates between about 0.85 and 0.98 are possible, with the most likely values again being in the middle of that range.

Because there are infinitely many possible rates, the heights in the figure do not correspond to probabilities. (If they did, the probabilities over all possible rates would sum to more than one). Instead, the heights of the posteriors correspond to probability densities, and they take values needed to ensure that the area under all of the densities is one. By themselves, these do not have meaning, but they do when compared or combined to other densities. In particular, the ratios between densities correspond to relative probabilities, so that if the density is twice as high at one rate as it is at another rate, then the first rate is twice as likely as the second. In addition, the area under the densities for a range of rates corresponds to the probability of that range. One consequence of the lack of absolute meaning for densities is that it is debatable whether the numerical values should be shown. The value 21 in the figure, for example, is not useful. It is the relative heights that convey information, and this is equally well done (with less visual clutter) by removing the -axis altogether.

Ranking sellers

Assessing which seller has the better underlying rate of positive reviews should incorporate the uncertainty measured and represented by the posterior distributions. One easily-interpreted way to do this is to estimate the probability that the rate for each seller is higher than the rate for each other seller. Intuitively, for example, the probability that the first seller is better than the second seller is the probability that a rate randomly sampled from the posterior distribution of the first seller is greater than a rate randomly sampled from the posterior distribution of the second seller. xxxxxxxxxx
# onLineSellers_2
# probability each seller is better than each other
model{
  for (i in 1:nSellers){
    # Data
    k[i] ~ dbin(theta[i], n[i])
    # Prior
    theta[i] ~ dunif(0, 1)
    # Probability each rate is greater than each other
    for (j in 1:nSellers){
      pi[i, j] = step(theta[i] - theta[j])
    }
  } 
}

The conclusion from these inferences is that the second seller is best. They have a 55% probability of having a greater underlying rate than the first seller, and a 71% probability of having a greater underlying rate than the second seller.

Posterior predictions

Another perspective on comparing the three sellers is provided by posterior prediction. What makes the sellers difficult to compare is the very different numbers of reviews they have received. In particular, the third seller has many more reviews, with . A posterior predictive analysis can address this discrepancy, by providing the distribution over the number of positive reviews the first two sellers would receive in the hypothetical situation in which they had also accumulated 200 reviews.

The JAGS model onlineSellers_3_jags.txt extends the original model to incorporate the posterior predictive analysis. The value of nPostpred corresponds to how many reviews are included in the hypothetical situation. In addition, piPostpred estimates the probability that each seller in this hypothetical situation receives more positive reviews than those actually received by the third seller.

xxxxxxxxxx
# onlineSellers_3
# posterior predictive of first two sellers relative to third
model{
  for (i in 1:nSellers){
    # Data
    k[i] ~ dbin(theta[i], n[i])
    # Prior
    theta[i] ~ dunif(0, 1)
    # Posterior predictive
    kPostpred[i] ~ dbin(theta[i], nPostpred)
    piPostpred[i] = step(kPostpred[i] - k[3])
  } 
}

The MATLAB script onLineSellers_3.m supplies the data to the model, and generates the plot of the posterior distributive distributions shown below. The top panel corresponds to the first seller, and the bottom panel corresponds to the second seller. In each panel, the bar graph shows the posterior predictive distribution of the seller, which specifies the probabilities that . Because the outcomes are a discrete set of possibilities, the heights of the bars can be interpreted as absolute probabilities. Within each panel, the observed count of positive reviews for the third seller is shown as a highlighted bar, showing how probable it would be for the first and second sellers.

The posterior predictive perspective analyses makes clear the uncertainty inherent in the original research question about which seller is best. There is a wide range of possible outcomes for the first seller, some of which are better than that observed for the third seller, and some of which are not. Once the first seller has received 200 reviews, the prediction, based on their first 10 reviews all being positive, is that they will have anywhere from about 120 to 200 positive reviews. For the second seller, the prediction is anywhere between about 160 and 200 positive reviews and so, again, could be better or worse than what was observed for the third seller.

This is a simple model, in which each seller has one underlying rate parameter that maps directly to the observed data counting positive reviews. This directness and simplicity means that the probabilities estimated using posterior prediction are essentially the same as those probabilities estimated from the underlying rates. In general, however, the two approaches to analysis are conceptually different. Posterior distributions are about latent psychological variables, and posterior predictive distributions are about observed data. In more complicated models with multiple parameters, this key conceptual difference will also lead to practical differences, and both approaches will be useful, depending on the research question. It may make more sense to think about whether one seller has a better underlying property, or whether its expected observed performance is superior.
