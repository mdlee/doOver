It is often convenient to formulate a model in terms of standard deviations rather than variances of precisions, because standard deviations are on the same scale as the mean, and it is usually easier to think on this scale to develop priors. For this model, we assume the mean height could be anywhere from 0 to 300 centimeters, so , and that the standard deviation could be anywhere from 0 to 100 centimeters, so .

The JAGS model heightGaussian_1_jags.txt implements the graphical model in a straightforward way. The one addition feature is the introduction of what we call the “posterior representative” for the data. It is defined as , and so is distributed in exactly the same way as the data. This means can be conceived as a new sample of data, based on the Gaussian that is inferred from the available data. In effect, in this case, can be thought of as the height of the next person to be observed (or, equivalently, of any random person who might be observed). In the JAGS script, corresponds to the variable yRep.

# Infer a Gaussian for height
model{
  for (i in 1:nPeople){
    y[i] ~ dnorm(mu, 1/sigma^2)
  }
  mu ~ dunif(0, 300)
  sigma ~ dunif(0, 100)
  # Representative
  yRep ~ dnorm(mu, 1/sigma^2)
}

It is important to understand that the and values that are paired come from the same posterior sample returned by JAGS. That is, these samples “belong together” as a potential account of the data. Each mean and standard deviation pair defines a Gaussian that is sampled in the posterior in proportion to the probability that it is the Gaussian that generated the data.

This key point of comprehension is unpacked in the figure by showing three specific examples. At the bottom right a joint posterior sample is highlighted that has a mean close to the mean of the observed data, and a relatively small standard deviation. The corresponding Gaussian is shown against the observed data, denoted by crosses. The Gaussian certainly could have generated the data, as all of the observations fall well within its regions of high density. At the center right a joint posterior sample is highlighted that has a mean close to the mean of the observed data, and standard deviation close to the standard deviation of the observed data. This Gaussian clearly could also have generated the data. Finally, at the top right a joint posterior sample is highlighted that has a mean larger than the mean of the observed data, and also has a relatively large standard deviation. This Gaussian could also have generated the data, but note that the relatively large standard deviation is needed, because the mean is not close to the sample mean. According to this possibility, the 10 observed heights all fell in the lower part of the true distribution. This, of course, is possible, and the inference provided by the joint posterior distribution is the set of all possibilities, generated in proportion to how likely they are given the data and the model.

The figure below shows the posterior distributions of and . It is important to keep these conceptually distinct. They are both useful, but they mean different things. The posterior for represents uncertainty about the mean height of the population of females from whom the data came. The posterior for represents the posterior uncertainty about the distribution of heights of that population of females. The first is where we expect the mean height to be. The second is where we expect the height of the next female to be.

It makes sense that the distribution for is much narrow. In general, it will become more peaked as more data are observed, and uncertainty about the decreases. The posterior representative distribution, in contrast, will generally not narrow as more data are observed. The additional data will increase certainty about both and , and the posterior representative distribution will converge towards the Gaussian represented by this and , but will continue to have the uncertainty associated with that Gaussian. Intuitively, more data helps us identify the mean, and the population distribution, but does not tell us where the next sample from that distribution will lie.
