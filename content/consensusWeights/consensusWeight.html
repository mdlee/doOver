Consensus Weight

This case-study involves data from a consensus estimation task (Lee & Shi, 2011). In the task, a group of three people are shown a household product, such as a toaster, along with a few sentences that summarize the features of the product. Each of the three people then estimates the cost of the product, to the nearest whole dollar. They did this one at a time, with all three people being able to hear the estimates of the others. Once all three people have made their individual estimates, the group then consults with each other to make a group consensus estimate for the product. The research question is how the final group consensus estimate relates to the three earlier individual estimates. The table below shows a few illustrative trials of the data from the experiment.

One possibility is that the group estimate is something like the average of the three individual estimates. Another possibility is that some individual estimates are given more weight that others. In particular, the Social Judgment Scheme model (Davis, 1996) assumes that estimates that are similar other estimates will be given more weight in determining the group consensus. This means, for example, that if people’s estimates were $15, $15, and $3, the final much lower estimate might not be given much weight in forming the consensus, and the group estimate might be closer to the consistent estimates of $15 rather than the mean of $11.

Inferring weights

The Lee and Shi (2011) experiment involved five groups of three people, with each group completing 50 estimation trials. We denote by the estimate of the th person in the th group on the th trial, and by the consensus estimate of the th group on the th trial.

We use the formalism of the Social Judgment Scheme model given by Ohtsubo, Masuchi, and Nakanishi (2002). The graphical model is shown below. Each group is treated as independent, and so there is an all-encompassing outer plate. Within this outer plate, there is a single node representing the latent decay for the group. The trials completed by each group then form an inner plate. On each trial, the estimates in the vector are observed and, together with the decay , determine to the vector of weights . Note that both the weights and estimates are denoted as deterministic: the estimates because they are fixed and not being modeled, and the weights because they are a deterministic function of the estimates and the decay. Finally, the weights and estimates then combine to model the observed consensus estimate for that group and trial.

The JAGS model consensusWeight_1_jags.txt implements the graphical model. It does this using what is sometimes called a “long” (as distinct from “wide”) approach to organizing the data. This means that all of the trials for all of the groups are appended, so that y is a vector of length totalTrials, and x is a matrix with totalTrials. The input vector g also has length totalTrials and g[t]indicates which of the five groups produced the estimates in y[t] and x[t, ]. This long form or organization is a general and useful one. Although not needed for the current data, it automatically extends to cases in which different groups complete different number of trials, or there are missing data. It simply represent in all of the observed trials, along with information like the group associated with each trials needed to implement the model. It is also worth noting that consensusWeight_1_jags.txt uses a temporary variable wTmp to breakdown the sums needed to define the actual weights w, and also uses yHat to calculate the predicted consensus estimate. Finally, the posterior predictive distribution is sampled by the variable yPostpred.

# Consensus weight
model{
  for (t in 1:totalTrials){
    y[t] ~ dnorm(yHat[t], 1)
    yPostpred[t] ~ dnorm(yHat[t], 1)
    yHat[t] = w[t, 1]*x[t, 1] + w[t, 2]*x[t, 2] + w[t, 3]*x[t, 3]
    w[t, 1] = wTmp[t, 1]/sum(wTmp[t, ])
    w[t, 2] = wTmp[t, 2]/sum(wTmp[t, ])
    w[t, 3] = wTmp[t, 3]/sum(wTmp[t, ])
    wTmp[t, 1] = f[t, 1, 2] + f[t, 1, 3]
    wTmp[t, 2] = f[t, 2, 1] + f[t, 2, 3]
    wTmp[t, 3] = f[t, 3, 1] + f[t, 3, 2]
    for (j in 1:3){
      for (k in 1:3){
        f[t, j, k] = exp(-theta[g[t]] * abs(x[t, j] - x[t, k])) 
      }
    }
  }
  for (i in 1:nGroups){
    theta[i] ~ dgamma(2, 1)
  }
}

it is clear that the decay rates are very small for each of the groups. Groups 2, 3, and 4 are inferred to have decay rates extremely similar to zero, while Groups 1 and 5 are inferred to have decay rates around 0.1.

Sometimes, posterior distributions that have most of their mass or density at the bounds of parameter space are an indication of weaknesses of a model. Prior distributions are intended to include values that will provide an adequate descriptions of the data. If the parameter values that lead to descriptive adequacy lie outside the bounds of a prior, it is often natural for the posterior to concentrate at the bounds, because those values are the best available.

Accordingly, it is especially important to check the descriptive adequacy of the Social Judgment Scheme model of consensus weights. A posterior predictive analysis is shown below. Each group corresponds to a panel, and each point within the panels to a trial completed by that group. The -axis corresponds to the behavioral data, in the form of the consensus estimate. The -axis corresponds to the posterior predictive distribution for that trial, with the circular marker showing the mean, and the error bars showing the 95% credible intervals. It is clear from this analysis that the model describes the data very well.

Comparison to averaging

The decay parameter inferences suggest that at least Groups 2, 3, and 4 might be better modeled by a simple arithmetic average, in which each estimate is given equal weight. Bayes factors address this sort of model selection question, by measuring how much more likely the observed data would be if one or the other model were true. Strictly, the and models are not nested, because the gamma distribution does not include 0. The key new addition is the binary latent-indicator parameter for the th group. The JAGS model consensusWeight_2_jags.txt implements the graphical model. The key addition is the z[i] parameter for each group, and the introduction of the two possible consensus predictions yHat[t, 1] and yHat[t, 2] for the two models. Note that because the z parameters take values 0 and 1, they need to be incremented by 1 to provide a valid index for the yHat variable, since JAGS begins with indices of 1.
